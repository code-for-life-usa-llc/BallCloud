Why some of Michigan’s top high school basketball players are leaving

Updated Aug 5, 2019; Posted Aug 5, 2019
Michigan basketball players to play at prep schools
13

Gallery: Michigan basketball players to play at prep schools

By Ryan Zuke | rzuke1@mlive.com

Some of the best high school boys basketball players are leaving Michigan, and the Michigan High School Athletic Association’s rule book may be a big reason why.

Nearly a dozen of the state’s top players opted to transfer out of state last year, including four-star guard Mark “Rocket” Watts, a Michigan State signee who left Old Redford for SPIRE Academy in Ohio.

The list of players fleeing Michigan reads like a who’s who of high school superstars.

· Isaiah Jackson, a 2020 five-star power forward, also transferred from Old Redford to SPIRE last year, although he transferred again to Waterford Mott in June.

· Terry Armstrong, the No. 5 shooting guard in the 2019 class nationally, left the Flint area multiple times before finishing his career up at Bella Vista College Prep in Arizona.

· Harlond Beverly, a 2019 University of Miami signee, departed Southfield Christian for Montverde Academy in Florida.

Academies and prep schools like SPIRE and Bella Vista can provide sport-specific training for high school-aged student-athletes, with a focus on preparing them athletically and academically for college. They do not operate under strict rule books and restrictions like most state high school associations.

The main reason top high school basketball players in Michigan are leaving for these types of schools, according to the former SPIRE coach who has lured some of the state’s best players to Ohio, is the MHSAA rule book.

Better competition and more time to focus on basketball are other key factors, he says.

"Basketball in the state of Michigan is an after-school activity," said Jermaine Jackson, the head coach at SPIRE last season who also spent two years coaching at Mt. Clemens High. "Why are we putting all of these rules on an after-school activity?

Emoni Bates has no plans to leave Lincoln for prep school

"I don't get the whole prep school thing," says his father, while the 2022 phenom takes aim at four state titles with Ypsilanti Lincoln.

Each state has its own governing body of high school athletics, but Jackson says the MHSAA's rules are stricter than others.

For instance, high school teams in Michigan can play a maximum of 20 regular-season games, while prep schools in other states can play more than double that.

Additionally, MHSAA schools are prohibited from playing teams that are not sanctioned by their state athletic association and they can’t travel more than 600 miles to play. Teams from outside of that 600-mile radius also are not be permitted to play at a Michigan high school.

The MHSAA’s new transfer rule, which forces a player to sit out a full season if he or she transfers to another in-state association high school without meeting a residency exception, also could affect players’ decisions in the future.

Another top basketball recruit from Michigan is reportedly transferring out of state

Carrington McCaskill, a forward from Detroit Renaissance expected to be a Mr. Basketball candidate, is the fourth Detroit player to transfer to SPIRE Academy in Ohio.

However, if a player transfers to an out of state prep school, they are eligible to play right away.

“Players feel like, in my eyes and a lot of guys', we feel like we’re under the radar because of the setbacks and the rules that we have,” said Detroit U-D Jesuit senior Julian Dozier, who played at Bella Vista his junior year before transferring back to U-D Jesuit so he could live with his mother. “I feel like being able to get out there and promote ourselves and things of that sort, it just helps us and it’s more fun. I feel like we like having fun playing basketball and where we have the most fun at is probably where we’ll go.”

Report: 4-star recruit Terry Armstrong of Flint to bypass college and turn pro

Armstrong had committed to Arizona before apparently reconsidering that decision.

Travel restrictions placed by other state athletic associations are also more lenient than the MHSAA.

For example, Ohio high schools can travel freely to play any team in a contiguous state, along with Ontario. They also can travel out of state once per season to a non-bordering state, as long as there is "no loss of school time." Playing against prep schools is permitted as well.

"The top kids are playing AAU (Amateur Athletic Union) right now anyway, but to be able to play a national schedule and go out and compete against the best talent in the country year-round is what high level kids want," said Ann Arbor Pioneer High Principal Tracey Lowder, whose son, Drew Lowder, played all four years at Pioneer and is signed to play at Holy Cross next season.

"There is nothing horribly wrong with how Michigan High School basketball is right now. There is talent all over the place, but when you start looking at the truly elite talents, they are wanting to play against the truly elite talents from around the country. In order to do that, you have to leave Michigan right now."

Meanwhile, the MHSAA says it cannot cater its rules to the best players.

“High schools sports is about the 99 percent,” MHSAA communications director John Johnson said. "For most kids, this is the biggest stage and last stage they will ever be on, so when people talk about the less than the 1 percent who seek greener pastures and perhaps better basketball opportunities, we have to stay true to our mission. We have to make sure we are here for nearly every other kid.

"To try and tilt the table in a way that caters to those who are already have gotten a lot of special handling in their life and are probably going to get a lot more, that is not fair to everyone else. It makes us look like club sports. It makes us look less educational."

Johnson also said there is a misconception of how the MHSAA governs. A school can propose a rule change at any time, which then would be reviewed by the respective sports committee. If it passes muster, then a recommendation can be made to the MHSAA 19-member representative council, which includes coaches, superintendents and school board members.

"They don't see a need to have so many more games or to let kids travel kids beyond where they can right now," Johnson said of membership schools. "When you look right now, we can have our kids travel to the western edges of Wisconsin and Illinois and down towards Cincinnati and over towards Cleveland and throughout Ontario.

"I think we can find plenty of competition in that big of an area. Once and a while you might hear about getting in an extra game or two on the schedule, but it is not an idea that has gotten a lot of traction, especially by administrators. Right now, for the most part, it appears schools are comfortable with the framework they set up."

Playing against top competition

Rules aside, proponents of prep schools say they can offer elite high school players an opportunity to face the best competition in the country.

In some circumstances, there could be 10 Division I recruits on the floor at the same time when top prep schools and academies square off.

"When you go to prep school, immediately, your practices become 10 times better every day because you are practicing with guys who are usually going to play Division I basketball," said Keith Wade, head coach of 1Nation Elite's 17U AAU team and assistant coach at Ann Arbor Skyline High. "There are always players better than you or equally as talented as you, and your stage is way bigger.

"If you haven't been recruited yet, then you are on a bigger stage where more colleges see you. All of your games give you that college feel because you are traveling, too."

At prep schools, players' schedules are centered around basketball. Most classes are completed online, and emphasis is mostly on test prep, such as the ACT and SAT. Some schools, such as SPIRE, have its athletes attend classes at local high schools, but it also has shorter days than regular students.

Justin Brantley, director at SPIRE, said his student-athletes have classes from 8 a.m. to 1:30 p.m. before a 30-minute study hall at the end of the day. They then have strength and conditioning from 2:30 to 4 p.m., followed by a second study hall focused on test prep.

Basketball players then practice from 5 to 7 p.m.

Overall, Brantley said, athletes are getting three to four hours of sport-specific training per day.

"It is one thing to get that training, and it is another to get it with people who really understand what they are doing," Brantley said. "Our strength and conditioning coach has a brilliant mind training high-profile athletes.

"Everything has a science to it. We are seeing our guys make huge strides in their speed and explosiveness and strength, and all of those things we are charting on a daily basis."

Former Southfield Christian head coach Josh Baker, who accepted a special assistant job under new Alabama head coach Nate Oats in April, had several players leave for prep schools during his eight-year tenure, including Beverly and 2015 three-star guard Bakari Evelyn, who is now at Valparaiso.

Watts tweeted on Wednesday that he had signed his letter of intent

Costs for prep schools/academies

Baker said he believes each player and their families have the right to do what is best for them. If a player aspires to play at the next level, and he believes prep school is the best route, Baker will support him.

"These guys are going to school for basketball," Baker said. "That's just the bottom line. Harlond (Beverly) is not going for his physics grade or because he's great at band. He's going because of basketball, and they're all smart enough to know that that gives them a free, elite education from some of the best universities in the world.

"You use that to benefit you. Just like the kid who is great on his ACT scores or the kid who is great at band, you have to do that."

Beverly said he left for Montverde to face better competition, and in doing so, his recruiting took off.

He already had a handful of offers before he moved, but while at Montverde, where he played alongside several other four-and five-star players, he received offers from Georgia, Kansas and Miami.

"At Montverde, every single night I had to play hard," Beverly said. "Back home, there are just not as many good players. We've got people from all over the country (at Montverde), so it's just different. It's a different atmosphere. Every night, I had to bring it because you could end up losing your spot to a freshman because they're all really good.

"I learned to play hard and make it habit. It just got me a lot better."'

Some prep schools have tuition costs that reach upwards of $60,000 per year, but Arnette Jordan, a scout for Prep Spotlight Magazine and former assistant coach at Kent State and Oakland University, estimated about 75 percent of athletes receive scholarships to attend. Backed by big donors and corporate sponsors, such as Nike, Under Armour and Adidas, prep schools can find a spot for elite talent for a minimal cost.

Beverly said it was cheaper for him to attend Montverde, which lists its tuition cost at $51,350 per year, than Southfield Christian.

"Southfield Christian didn't give any athletic scholarships," Beverly said. "Schools like (Montverde) are allowed to."

Impact on recruiting

Last season, SPIRE was one of the top basketball programs in the country with a 31-2 record. Brantley said at least nine players had Division I offers as about 120 college coaches came to watch practice during the season.

"Without a doubt, their exposure went through the roof," Brantley said.

Jordan said colleges will recruit talented players no matter where they play, but with NCAA restrictions on recruiting, college coaches are limited on how many games they can attend.

"They are going to go where the talent is, but they like going to prep schools and prep school tournaments because they get a chance to see kids compete at a high level, like a college game," Jordan said. "They are able to judge them playing in a college atmosphere and predict how a kid may be able to perform (at the college level)."

Athletes who play high school basketball for all four years also get ample opportunities to play at the college level. This past season, 16 players from Michigan and Michigan State's rosters came from MHSAA-sanctioned schools.

Two of Wade's sons had opportunities to play at prep schools but both opted to finish their careers at Skyline. Brandon Wade played last season at Duquesne and is currently in the NCAA transfer portal, while Ryan Wade is signed to play at Holy Cross next year.

The Wades were able to play in front of the community they grew up in, and even had an undefeated regular season in 2017-18. Ryan also said they received enough exposure playing on a national level during the spring and summer AAU seasons.

AAU provides similar competition as the elite academies, and nearly all college prospects, even those at state-sanctioned high schools, play AAU, which runs from about April until July.

"The biggest thing for me was, growing up in Ann Arbor, which is such a beautiful city, it was a thing where, we went in there and built a program together. Me, my brother and everybody. It was a home, family feeling. I wanted to stay true to Ann Arbor."

Jordan agrees that high school players have an opportunity to get more exposure on AAU circuits but said there are only so many spots on the most high-profile teams in the country.

"There are only a certain amount of slots to play for The Family, REACH and the Under Armour team," Jordan said. "If you don't get one of those slots, then you might not be playing on the national level."

Possible solutions

Jordan thought he was going to be a part of history in changing the landscape of high school basketball in Michigan.

He had some of the top basketball players in the state lined up to play at the Lead International Academy at the Legacy Center Sports Complex in Brighton, which was going to be the state's first prep school when it was scheduled to open for the 2017-18 school year.

The school had been working with Michigan Virtue to get its online course work accredited to become NCAA compliant. It also had a deal in place with Apple to provide technology such as phone and computers for the student-athletes. A schedule for the basketball team was close to being completed in preparation of the opening.

But about a month before LIA was scheduled to open, it did not get the necessary funding it needed. Lowder, who was one of about 30 involved in trying the project, said the MHSAA's rules also played a factor.

"It was going to be very difficult to get it going," Lowder said. "If Legacy built a place we could host games, which was a part of a plan, we could host schools there and play there, but other than that, we couldn't play (at other Michigan high schools). Schoolcraft college was looking to help us out, but then you get into eligibility issues by kids playing on a junior college campus."

Lowder and Jordan still believe a prep school in Michigan is necessary but said the MHSAA rules need to change for it to be viable.

"The MHSAA would have to be willing to help out with some of the things that are not permissible," Lowder said. "You start looking at the travel guidelines, you can only go so far on a certain day and can only play so many games in a week. If the MHSAA were able to loosen some of those things up, it would make it a lot easier.

"There is an opportunity there, though (to open a prep school in Michigan), and somebody is going to jump in and take it at some point. When they do, it is going to blow up because there is such a great need. People are clamoring for it."

MLive sports reporter Jared Purcell contributed to this story.
